The Relationship Between Caffeine Use and GPA Averages:

Description:

College students consume surprising quantities of caffeine on average. This study seeks to determine if a correlation exists between the amount of caffeine an individual consumes daily and that individual's grade point average in school. The initial experiment included a group, which received daily caffeine equal to the national average (Group A), a group which received twice the national daily average (Group B), and a control group which receive a placebbo (Group C). Preliminary results indicate that average consumption of daily caffeine led to a slight and steady increase in GPA throughout the term. A higher than average consumption of caffeine led to higher GPA initially, but then grades fell off towards the end of the semester. Finally, no consumption of caffeine led to neither a significant increase or decrease in overall GPA performance.

Steps to Reproduce:

  1. 100 students were sleected from volunteer applicants between the ages of 18-22 years old, with equal numbers of female and male participants with a starting GPA between 3.2 and 3.3.
  2. Each student was assigned a number from 1-100.
  3. Numbers were then randomly selected and assigned to groups A, B, and C.
  4. Group A was given caffeine equivalent to 3 cups of coffee per day (the national average).
  5. Group B was given caffeine equivalent to 6 cups of coffee per day.
  6. Group C was given a placebo.
  7. Grade point averages were tracked throught the term and averaged by group at the start of the semester, at midterm, and after final grades were submitted.

Data Results:

Group Number: Starting Average: Midterm Average: Final Average:
Group A: 3.25 3.30 3.31
Group B: 3.30 3.62 3.14
Group C: 3.28 3.27 3.30
